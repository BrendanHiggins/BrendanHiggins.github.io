I wrote a book. It's not published yet, and when it is I'll probably write a totally separate post about it. I may end up self-publishing, but I wanted to try the more difficult path first and attempt to get traditionally published.

Enter the world of _Literary Agents_.   

*A Quick Disclaimer Before We Begin*
I'm going to reference a specific agent's question in this post (who has a stellar track record and a great client roster), and in the event they ever see it I want to be clear that this isn't intended to be an attack on that specific agency/agent. Rather, this style of question can be found on a great number of agents query/submission pages. So while the example is specific, it's indicative of the larger problem. It's a systemic trend across the entire industry and that is what I'll be discussing in this post.

## A Slippery Slope: AI Usage Disclosure
As mentioned above, I saw this style of AI disclosure on many agents query forms, but this one in particular stood out to me so I'll use it as the primary example for the rest of this post.
> We stand against the use of AI to generate prose. If AI was used for research, plotting, world building, or any other task related to writing the manuscript or preparing submission materials, please disclose how it was used and to what extent.
_0/1000 words to explain yourself in a text box_

I find this sort of question out of touch, and frankly, unrealistic for authors to answer with every passing year. AI development is rapid, and AI integration into services/products is becoming, and will continue to become, ubiquitous whether we like it or not. 

Heck, I'm writing this blog post in an IDE right now that autofill’s my next words and lets me hit TAB if I want it to complete the entire sentence for me. It's not even a program marketed for writing, it's software for writing code but it can write a convincing enough sentence that I'll could occasionally just hit TAB and move on.

And thus, we begin the problems...

My general premise for this post is simple: Agents demanding AI disclosure is problematic because it possibly punishes the wrong people. Furthermore, the definition of "AI" is loose, and poorly understood. To compound this, even the term "use" is subjective and may vary wildly from person to person.

## The Implication - "Zero AI Tolerance"
The most reasonable interpretation of this request is that agents want to know if AI was used to generate prose. Or, in less specific terms, they want to know if your actual writing, the sentences, paragraphs, etc. are AI generated. I interpret this to be _wholesale_ generated.

That's a fairly reasonable stance to take, but we can get into the specifics on why even this becomes problematic. 

The more problematic aspect of this is the 'research, plotting, world building, or ANY OTHER TASK' bit. First of all, ANY OTHER TASK is the most overly broad thing I can conceive, but sure we'll go with it.

Let's say the agent's actual stance was: no AI use, in any form or the manuscript is invalid.

Well, congrats, your only clients are cavemen and George R.R. Martin who writes on a typewriter in his loft. If anyone even Google's a question, the first thing they see is an LLM (Gemini) generated answer for that question.

So if you ever searched 1 thing in relation to your novel on Google (or Bing!), you're out. Sorry.

And now we see the problem. What constitutes 'use' and when does the line get drawn for disqualification for utilization of technology? 

Let's give a few more examples. The tool [Grammarly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammarly) came out in 2009, long before "AI" (LLMs). But today? The first thing you see on their page is that it's an _AI-powered writing assistant_. Which it clearly is after years in development, but what if you only used it for basic things like spelling, grammar, or thesaurus suggestions? Does that qualify as use of an AI tool? 

To make things even more blurry, consider Microsoft Word's spellcheck. It's been around for ages, but today? It's AI-powered. Same is true for Google docs. Not to mention, both word editors have their brand of AI baked directly into the product: Gemini and Copilot. 

Use the proofread or editor functions of your software? Sorry, you used AI. You are disqualified.

## The Honesty Penalty
Many people, probably the vast majority of people, are actually pretty ignorant when it comes to "AI." It's a term much like "Cloud" that means nothing to them. If you say LLM, they look at you confused. This is almost certainly true for many creative people as well, who are not necessarily tech savvy or interested in the inner workings of the technology behind the tools they use. 

But for me, as a technologist, I'm very familiar with the tools I use. Which means when someone asks a question like "How much AI did you use in your book?" I have to stop and think.

Well... I Googled about 10,000 things for my book. So I guess I used AI 10,000 times?

But someone who has no clue what is AI and what isn't AI would never have this thought. They would just hit NONE and move on. 

The core dilemma here is trying to interpret the Agent's intent with the question. 

What is their tolerance? It's unclear. 
What is their definition of AI? It's unclear.
What is their definition of "use"? It's unclear.

If you lie about your AI use, you are a liar.
If you don't lie, and are honest, will the agent baselessly reject you because you admitted to using AI tools? 

## The Blurry Line of "Assistance" and The Ship of Theseus
Let's write a story together. This example will examine the _prose_ angle which is the most fair interpretation of what the agent is trying to weed out of submissions.

> Bob is a builder. He lives in a beautiful home with his wife and three children. Bob aspires to be an author. Each night, once his family is fast asleep he slips out of bed and downstairs to his office. Behind his desk he sits in the darkness, illuminated only by the glow of the display. He types. He writes his story. It's going to be a grand, beautiful story.

I'll just be honest, I wrote this in 30 seconds and made it up as I went. It's not a great story. So let's have AI re-write it, we'll use Gemini 3 Fast.

*Prompt:* Please rewrite this story to be a bit more polished and literary.

> By day, Bob shaped the physical world with wood and stone. He had constructed a good life, anchored by an elegant home that hummed with the chaotic warmth of his wife and three children. Yet, a different kind of architecture occupied his mind. Every night, after the steady, quiet rhythm of deep sleep settled over the bedrooms, he would ease himself from the mattress and descend the stairs in silence. The downstairs office became his sanctuary, a pocket of absolute darkness broken only by the cold, pale glow of the monitor painting his face. He sat behind the desk and rested his heavy hands on the keys. Then, he typed. Keystroke by keystroke, he built a new world, laying the foundation for a narrative he knew would be vast, magnificent, and entirely his own.

Wow, uh yeah...that's overkill. Let's tweak it by hand.

> By day, Bob was a builder. His hands worked with wood and stone, and he had built a good life by doing so. He had a modest home, and a beautiful family. Each night, he would return home to his wife and three children. Yet, a different type of work occupied his mind. Every night, after the family had fallen into the quiet rhythm of sleep, he would slip out of bed and head downstairs to his office. The office was where he would write his novel. Illuminated by the glow of his monitor, he would type away the midnight hours. He aspired to be an author, and nothing would stop him. Just as by day he constructed buildings, by night he was constructing entire worlds. It brought Bob immense joy.

I like this more, maybe you do too. And I did it on the fly in ~2 minutes. I did it by taking the output from the prompt above, and dialing it way back by hand. 

And once more, let's iterate.

*Prompt:* This is nearly right, don't change much. Keep it almost identical, just spruce it up a bit.

> By day, Bob was a builder. His hands worked with wood and stone, and through them, he had built a good life. He had a modest home and a beautiful family. Each evening, he would return home to his wife and three children. Yet, a different type of work occupied his mind. Every night, after the house had fallen into the quiet rhythm of sleep, he would slip out of bed and head downstairs to his office. This was where he wrote his novel. Illuminated by the glow of his monitor, he would type away the midnight hours. He aspired to be an author, and nothing would stop him. Just as by day he constructed buildings, by night he constructed entire worlds. It brought Bob immense joy.

And, as you can see...it's barely different. So now we have four versions of a story. The first, 100% human. The second, arguably 100% AI, although it has been prompted by the 100% human version that's hard to interpret. The third, modified by human. The final, modified by AI.

Which part is human? Which part is AI?

Nobody can really say. Maybe it's 50/50, maybe it's 25/75...maybe it's 75/25. It's impossible to say for sure. What if you did this 4 more times? You have a telephone-game of prose.

In a way, if you utilize AI like this, you create your own literary [Ship of Theseus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ship_of_Theseus). It's not a perfect analogy, but it's close enough to be useful.

You can plug the text into an AI "detector" and it will flag it, of course. But these services are wildly unreliable, and often plain wrong. Not to mention, they are incentivized to flag the text as non-human so they can sell you their service to 'humanize' the text (with an AI ironically). 

For example, one website flagged the Bob story text as 92% AI...but we just showed that to be false, I wrote well over 50% of it, even if we're being conservative. 

To further prove my point, I submitted 2 paragraphs from chapter 1 of Jurassic Park (the novel) and it returned ... 82% AI. Like I said, _very_ accurate. Michael Crichton was ahead of his time with LLM use in 1990.

## Prompting as a Skill
Here's where we run into another issue. Prompting an AI is a skill in and of itself. It's not something that everyone is good at. It takes practice to get good at prompting an AI. 

You cannot go to your favorite AI and simply submit: Please write me a compelling mystery novel.

It just doesn't work that way. Although there's enough obvious AI slop self-published these days that you can tell people are trying this. And it's clear their skill in prompting is poor.

With that being said, you can work with an AI to help you brainstorm ideas, characters, plot points, and more. Furthermore, you could use AI like an editor helping you smooth out sentences and awkward sections of your manuscript.

Being 'good' at prompting is an augmentation of the skillset of writing. It should not replace your skills or replace your imagination. It's simply a tool.

Nobody judges a woodworker for using a table saw. It's the expectation. If every woodworker was expected to use a handsaw for each cut we'd live in a very different world. Yet, that's seemingly the expectation for writers.

## The Literary Panic
In a way, I get it. The literary world likely views AI as an existential threat. As it improves, why do you need a proof reader? Why do you need a beta reader? Why do you need a developmental editor? Why do you need a co-writer? 

AI can and will be able to do all those things.

Why do you need an agent at all? You can self publish fairly easily these days and many have found success by doing so.

This is quite different from the way the tech industry views AI. Yes, it's an existential threat in the tech industry as well. I think about how AI might 'take my job' every other week. Even so, in the tech world, if I come up with a way to use AI to make my job easier, or to make my code better, It's seen as a success.

Most places have metrics for how much AI you can possibly use, and they encourage it. It's a tool for efficiency, not a threat to be banned.

Even in non-tech industries like Hollywood they are beginning to adopt AI to generate concept art, and even replace visual effects where they can.

Which is why it's quite interesting that the literary world is reacting so differently. If the prose is good, the plot is sound, the story is fun, and it reads naturally, does the tool matter?

I read a fair bit. At least 1 book a month. I don't want to purposely read 'AI slop' ... but I'm also not over analyzing my books to try to figure out if AI was involved in their creation or not. Of course, if it was published prior to 2020 we're basically 100% safe to say it wasn't AI generated. 

As a reader, I don't really care about the process. I care about the resulting story and if the writing is solid.

## Copyrights and Gatekeeping
Of course, there is a lot of nuance here. An underlying reason for the industries general aversion or apprehension about AI generated text is the legal question. Text that was purely, or primarily generated by AI may have issues with copyright. It's a very fuzzy, grey area of copyright law right now.

The second fear of course, is that an agent accepts bad AI work and then pushes it off on a reader base who notices because it's legitimately bad, and the agent is now seen as less legitimate in the industry. It's a reputational risk.

All that being said, the reality is that "banning" AI from the literary world is like trying to ban the printing press. It's not going to happen. Unless every author you sign is some strange hermit who refuses to use a computer, you're going to have AI influence in your books. 

Plus, it's a form of gatekeeping. Many people who aspire to be authors cannot afford $1,000+ to hire a developmental editor or even $200 for a beta reader. AI can provide them facsimiles of these things for a $20/month subscription. 

If someone is an amazing writer, but their second language is English, AI can help them write in English. Is that a bad thing? I would argue it is not.

Finally, this is not going away. Pandora's box is already opened whether the literary world likes it or not. As younger generations grow up with AI, their perception of acceptable use with shift the entire landscape with time. 

What do you mean did I use AI? Obviously I used AI, I've been using it since I was in the 5th grade. 
Of course I had AI edit my book? Why wouldn't I? I'm not an English major, I'm a construction worker with a dream.

## The Unavoidable Future
As I continue my journey into querying agents I'll have to keep my pessimistic view of the literary world and its seemingly unrealistic views of AI out of the spotlight. I'm sure It won't be well received, but the reality is, I'm fairly confident about everything I wrote here.

A smart agent would define what they're comfortable with, and work with authors to make sure they're comfortable with it too. Agents are partners with authors, and partnership should include discussion of tools and techniques. What is acceptable use? When is the use of AI too much? 

By just asking "did you use AI" you are alienating a growing portion of the writing community. It's also such a vague and somewhat ignorant question.

Hopefully as the technology matures, the literary world will mature with it. But for now, this is the world we live in.

That being said, if anyone asks did AI write my book? No. I did. 

This whole post probably comes across as defensive: this guy clearly wrote his whole book with AI and now feels like he has to write a big blog post justifying it. Truth is, I've been writing my book of over two years and haven't used AI for much more than the occasional sentence suggestion, as well as research for the historical/science aspects of the novel. But as I query agents, I can't help but feel guilty.
